Meta · Forum

Vibe-coding a new app for Facebook groups

Role
Lead Content Designer
Collaborators
Data Science, Engineering, Legal, Product Design, UX Research
Key outcome
Shipped in 4 months, received major positive press coverage
AI-native design Content design Information architecture
Overview

Facebook's main app is cluttered and unfocused. For group admins and power users, the tools they rely on most for their groups are buried in an app built to do everything for everyone.

This led to Forum, a standalone app dedicated to Facebook Groups. In just 4 months, I was part of the team that built it from scratch and shipped it publicly. I was the lead content designer for the admin experience, responsible for the admin AI product and the admin section architecture.

I had to honour the mental models from established Facebook patterns, while modernising the experience and designing an AI-powered assistant with no established precedent to follow.

Process

I audited the existing Facebook admin experience, then worked with PM, PD, UXR, and DS to decide what to carry over to Forum and what to leave behind.

For Admin AI, I owned:

  • The full prompt system, simplifying 100+ admin/moderation actions. I prioritised the prompts using live usage data and refined through testing.
  • AI response rules and guidelines, ranking logic, and personalisation based on group status and admin behaviour.
  • A follow-up question system to help admins keep moving instead of hitting a dead end after their initial question.

Our team worked AI-natively throughout, using Claude, Figma Make, VS Code, and Cursor to design, prototype, and ship. Human-led process, moving faster with AI tools.

Naming the AI feature

I tested three directions with ~150 of Facebook's most active admins: "admin AI," "admin AI assistant," and "admin agent."

  • Admin AI assistant caused confusion alongside the existing Admin Assist tool.
  • Admin agent tested poorly. "Agent" isn't an everyday term for most users, and the tool isn't truly agentic, making the label both unclear and inaccurate.
  • Admin AI won.
My thinking

Generic and lowercase on purpose. It sets honest expectations today and preserves the flexibility to merge Admin AI and Admin Assist under a unified name later, without requiring a re-education moment for admins.

Pill prompt system
My thinking

Instead of an open chat interface, I built a contextual system of 100+ pill prompts personalised by group type, maturity, privacy model, admin structure, and pending actions. I also set a grammatical rule: verb-first pills ("Draft welcome post") signal the AI is acting; noun-first pills ("Growth trends") signal it's surfacing information. One rule, scannable at a glance.

Outcomes

The app launched in May 2026, receiving coverage from TechCrunch, The Next Web, The Verge, CNET, and many others.

Manulife Bank

Building self-service tools to reduce inbound calls

Role
Content Designer
Collaborators
Compliance, Engineering, Product Design
Key result
Reduced call centre volume by 13%
Content design Service design Voice & tone
Overview

Manulife Bank's call centre was routinely overwhelmed, and customers were frustrated waiting on hold just as often. The kicker: a large portion of incoming calls were for questions, problems, and transactions customers could have resolved themselves in online banking or the mobile app.

The challenge was that the customers most likely to call in were the least likely to be mobile-savvy. Introducing a new in-app channel meant we couldn't just build it. We had to bring those customers along with us.

Process

Intercept with self-serve options

My thinking

I developed and ranked the items on the Subject dropdown based on the most common inbound call reasons, most of which already linked directly to self-serve actions available in the app.

When a customer selects a subject like "I need a certified/official cheque," we surface a "Good news!" prompt, letting them complete the transaction immediately on their own.

In addition to deflecting the call, we're teaching customers what they could do independently.

Harder cases: account closures

My thinking

For account closures, we removed friction from the early steps rather than adding it. We surfaced a checklist of things to cancel before closing (pre-authorised payments, cheques, direct deposits) to protect customers from NSF charges, and to warm them up for a retention conversation once the rep was involved.

Outcomes
13% Decrease in call centre requests for self-serve transactions

Reducing call centre requests by 13% was a big win, but the bigger win was changing behaviour, teaching and empowering customers to complete actions on their own, rather than defaulting to calling in for help.

Questrade

Creating Canada's first robo-advisor

Role
Content Designer
Collaborators
Compliance, Data Science, Portfolio Management, Product Design
Key outcome
Beat new accounts goal by 60%
Content design Legal & regulation Voice & tone
Overview

Questrade is Canada's leader in self-directed trading, but many Canadians don't want to invest on their own. Robo-advisors were growing in other countries, letting people automate their investing without paying high bank fees, but there weren't any in Canada at the time.

We were tasked with building the onboarding system for robo-advisor clients: simple, educational, and designed for people who find the investing world overwhelming.

Tone and language
My thinking

Despite working at a stock brokerage, I wasn't an investor. That put me in a great position to write for novice users. I made the tone deliberately conversational, phrasing things casually to build a relationship with users, while including proper definitions to maintain credibility.

Writing knowledge options like "Investments? Financial markets? My head is spinning" instead of "I'm a novice" wasn't just tone: it was a signal that this product was built for them.

Conflicting answers
My thinking

We dug into users' financial goals and risk tendencies, and flagged any conflicts when their answers didn't line up, to make sure we matched them with the portfolio best-suited to their needs.

Regulators were actively developing guidance on robo-advisors during the build, requiring content revisions on the fly. I worked closely with compliance throughout to adapt as the rules moved, without degrading the user experience.

Outcomes
60% Above new accounts target in year one
10% Above total asset goal in year one
#1 Consistently ranked among Canada's top robo-advisors

Meta · Facebook Groups

Letting groups safely change privacy settings

Role
Lead Content Designer
Collaborators
Engineering, Legal, Product Design, UX Research
Key outcome
270K+ private groups converted to public
Content design Legal & regulation Privacy
Overview

In 2019, the FTC and Meta agreed on a privacy consent order, blocking the ability to let private groups become public. This limited a group's ability to grow, strained admin resources, and prevented helpful content from being discovered through organic search.

The reverse was also a problem: public groups going private were quietly draining Facebook's organic search ecosystem of discoverable content.

My thinking

The key was a structural asymmetry: new content goes public, but historical content stays private. My job was to make that genuinely complex state legible to everyday admins and members, in a way that satisfied the FTC order.

The admin and member experience

The group admin's experience

The group member's experience

Three interlocking pieces
  • Transparent conversion guardrails: Explicit education and UI telling admins what will change and what won't.
  • A 3-day review window: When an admin initiates a conversion, co-admins are alerted and have 3 days to cancel. After the conversion, members are notified so they can decide whether to stay or leave.
  • Defensive action to keep groups public: Ask admins what problem they're trying to solve, and pair them with existing tools to address it.
My thinking

Getting this done meant holding three competing interests in tension at once: what users needed, what regulators required, and what the business wanted. My experience navigating legal and compliance review meant I could push hard on all three without losing any of them.

Outcomes
270K+ Groups converted to public
1,200+ Large public groups retained via admin tool education
24 Tier-1 media outlets covered the launch

Coverage from TechCrunch, Lifehacker, and others specifically noted that Meta had found a way to give admins growth tools without exposing members' historical private posts, a distinction that came entirely from the content architecture.
