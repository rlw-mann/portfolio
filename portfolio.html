Meta · Forum

A new app for Facebook group admins

Role
Lead Content Designer
Collaborators
Data Science, Engineering, Legal, Product Design, UX Research
AI-native design Content design Information architecture
Problem

Facebook's main app is cluttered and unfocused. For group admins and power users, the tools they rely on most for their groups are buried in an app built to do everything for everyone.

Solution

This led us to create Forum, a standalone app dedicated to Facebook Groups. Forum gives group admins and power users their own space, built for the people who make communities run.

In 4 months, we built Forum from scratch and shipped the app publicly.

My role

I was the lead content designer for the admin experience on Forum, working with two other CDs who covered different sections of the app. I developed the admin AI product and the admin section architecture.

As the Lead CD, I had to honour the mental models from established Facebook patterns, while modernizing the experience and designing an AI-powered assistant with no established precedent to follow.

Process

I audited the existing Facebook admin experience, then worked with PM, PD, UXR, and DS to decide what to carry over to Forum and what to leave behind.

For Admin AI, I owned:

  • The full prompt system, simplifying 100+ admin/moderation actions. I prioritized the prompts using live usage data, and refined through testing.
  • AI response rules and guidelines, ranking logic, and personalization based on group status and admin behaviour.
  • A follow-up question system to help admins keep moving instead of hitting a dead end after their initial question.

Our team worked AI-natively throughout, using Claude, Figma Make, VS Code, and Cursor to design, prototype, and ship. Human-led process, moving faster with AI tools.

Naming

I tested three directions with ~150 of Facebook's most active admins: "admin AI," "admin AI assistant," and "admin agent."

  • Admin AI assistant caused confusion alongside the existing Admin Assist tool.
  • Admin agent tested poorly — "agent" isn't a term everyday users use, and the tool isn't truly agentic, making the label both unclear and inaccurate.
  • Admin AI won: generic, lowercase, honest about what it does today, and flexible enough to merge with Admin Assist later without re-educating admins.

The result was a deliberately generic, lowercase label that sets honest expectations today and preserves the flexibility to merge Admin AI and Admin Assist under a unified name later, without requiring a re-education moment for admins.

Pill prompt strategy

Instead of an open chat interface, I built a contextual system of 100+ pill prompts personalized by group type, maturity, privacy model, admin structure, and pending actions.

I also set a grammatical rule for how pills are written: verb-first pills ("Draft welcome post") signal the AI is acting; noun-first pills ("Growth trends") signal it's surfacing information. This kept the interface scannable and set clear expectations at a glance.

Manulife Bank

Secure in-app inbox to reduce inbound calls

Role
Lead content designer
Collaborators
Compliance, Engineering, Product Design
Content design Service design Voice & tone
Problem

Manulife Bank's call centre was routinely overwhelmed, and customers were frustrated waiting on hold just as often. The kicker: a large portion of incoming calls were for questions, problems, and transactions customers could have resolved themselves in online banking or the mobile app.

The challenge was that the customers most likely to call in were the least likely to be mobile-savvy. Introducing a new in-app channel meant we couldn't just build it. We had to bring those customers along with us.

Process

I designed the content system for a new secure in-app inbox, including a pre-filled Subject dropdown built from the most common inbound call reasons. Most of those subjects linked directly to self-serve actions already available in the app.

When a customer selected a subject like "I need a certified/official cheque," we surfaced a "Good news!" prompt letting them complete the transaction themselves, no message needed. This educated customers about what they could do independently while deflecting the call.

For harder cases, like account closures, we removed friction from the early steps rather than adding it. We surfaced a checklist of things to cancel before closing (pre-authorized payments, cheques, direct deposits) to protect customers from NSF charges, and to warm them up for a retention conversation once the rep was involved.

Outcomes

13% decrease in call centre requests for self-serve transactions.

The harder win wasn't the number. It was teaching a segment of customers who had only ever called the bank that they didn't have to.

Questrade

Building Questrade's robo-advisor

Role
Content Designer
Collaborators
Compliance, Data Science, Portfolio Management, Product Design
Compliance Content design Voice & tone
Problem

Questrade is Canada's leader in self-directed trading, but there are so many Canadians that don't want to invest on their own.

There was a new concept of robo-advisors growing in other countries, where people can automate their investing without paying high bank fees, but there weren't any in Canada at the time.

Solution

We were tasked with creating an onboarding system for robo-advisor clients. Knowing that Canadians who would opt for a robo-advisor were less knowledgeable about the investment world, we needed to make it as simple and educational as possible, to empower our new clients and ensure they were comfortable while entering the often-overwhelming world of investing.

Process

Despite working at a stock brokerage, I wasn't at all an investor. This put me in a great position to make sure the content was clear and easy to understand for even the most novice investors.

I deliberately made the tone more conversational, phrasing things casually to help build a relationship with users, while including proper definitions and explanations to maintain credibility as a trusted manager for your investments.

We also dug into their financial goals and tendencies, and flagged any conflicts when their answers didn't line up, to make sure we matched them with a portfolio best-suited to fit their needs.

Writing investment knowledge options like "Investments? Financial markets? My head is spinning" instead of "I'm a novice" wasn't just tone; it was a deliberate strategy to meet first-time investors where they were, and signal that this product was built for them.

Regulators were actively developing guidance on robo-advisors during the build, requiring content revisions on the fly to meet requirements without degrading the user experience. I worked closely with compliance throughout to adapt as the rules moved.

Outcomes
  • Beat new accounts target by 60% in year one.
  • Beat total asset goal by 10% in year one.
  • Consistently ranked as one of Canada's top robo-advisors.

Meta · Facebook Groups

Letting groups safely change privacy settings

Role
Lead Content Designer
Collaborators
Engineering, Legal, Product Design, UX Research
Content design Legal & regulation Privacy
Problem

In 2019, the FTC and Meta agreed on a privacy consent order, blocking the ability to let private groups become public.

This limits a group's ability to switch from private to public, impacting their growth, straining group admin resources, and blocking helpful group content from being discovered through organic search.

The reverse was also a problem: public groups going private were quietly draining Facebook's organic search ecosystem of discoverable content.

Solution

The key was a structural asymmetry: new content goes public, but historical content stays private.

My job was to make that genuinely complex state legible to everyday admins and members, in a way that satisfied the FTC order.

To make it work, I designed three interlocking pieces:

  • Transparent conversion guardrails: Explicit education and UI, telling admins what will change and what won't.
  • A 3-day review window: When an admin initiates a conversion, co-admins are alerted and have 3 days to cancel the switch. After the conversion, members are notified so they can decide if they want to stay or leave the group.
  • Defensive action to keep groups public: Ask admins what problem they're trying to solve, and pair them with existing tools to address the issue.

All of this was done in close partnership with legal and regulatory partners, ensuring we served business and product goals, while respecting user privacy.

Outcomes
  • 270K+ groups converted to public (128% of primary goal).
  • 1,200+ large public groups retained via interception.
  • 24 tier-1 media outlets covering the launch.

Coverage from TechCrunch, Lifehacker, and others specifically noted that Meta had found a way to give admins growth tools without exposing members' historical private posts, a distinction that came entirely from the content architecture.

Meta · Facebook Games

Driving ~$50M/year with two new games features

Role
Content & Product Designer
Collaborators
Data Science, Engineering, Legal
Content design Monetization Product design
Context

Facebook Games is a unique part of the app, in that it has two distinct audiences: people who spend time playing games on Facebook, and developers who build the games.

My team's job was to grow both sides, helping developers make a living with their games, and helping people take a break or connect with others over games they love.

On these two products, I was both the Content and Product Designer, owning the words and pixels of each experience. Collectively, these products earn ~$50M USD per year.

Project 1: Notifications as a service

Problem

If you've ever played a game on Facebook, you've probably also gotten annoying reminders asking you to come back and play again. This was because game developers would write and send their own notifications, and the result is people turn off notifications altogether, and stop playing games.

But it's not the fault of the game developers. They make great games, but they aren't lifecycle marketers, so writing, scheduling, and targeting notifications isn't their strong suit.

Unfortunately, bad content makes people turn off notifications and stop playing games.

Solution

To solve this, I proposed a new product, Notifications as a Service, where we would set content standards and guardrails, then handle notification targeting. This helped in two key ways:

1. New standards meant the content had to hit a quality threshold before being sent.

2. Facebook handles the targeting, so notifications reach the right players at the right time.

The design problem was developer trust. Studios were handing over a direct channel to their players, so the product had to make it legible what would be sent, when, and why, and make the value obvious enough that giving up manual control felt like a win rather than a loss.

Results

  • Better notification quality, minimal truncation, and consistent notification structure.
  • More time spent playing, as Facebook's targeting found the right people at the right time.
  • Revenue: $32M+ per year via ads, from a 3% increase in time spent playing.
Project 2: In-app purchases on iOS

Problem

For years, Apple didn't allow in-app purchases (IAP) in Facebook Games, as it competed with their iOS in-game purchases. This meant games on Facebook were missing elements and features that would let players buy upgrades to improve their gameplay experience.

However, the games did have IAP functionality on Facebook on Android and web, which meant game developers had to build unique, separate versions of their games for Facebook on iOS, despite making less money from iPhone users.

Solution

Thanks to legal changes in 2024, we could finally integrate IAP into our games, allowing developers to expand their games with new functionality and features to match the experience on other platforms.

With the change, the launch had two audiences with opposite needs: developers who had to adopt and integrate the feature, and players who had to trust a purchase flow they hadn't seen on Facebook before.

My role

I created all the content for both sides of that launch: the developer-facing material that drove adoption, and the player-facing content that made the purchase experience feel safe and expected. The launch was covered by PocketGamer.biz.

Results

  • A better, consistent game experience across all platforms.
  • Revenue: $16.5M per year.
Outcomes
~$50M Combined annual revenue across both products
$88K Daily ad revenue from Notifications as a Service
$45K Daily revenue from iOS in-app purchases
